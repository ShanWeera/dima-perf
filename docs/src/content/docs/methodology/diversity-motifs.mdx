Diversity motifs are the core analytical feature that distinguishes DiMA from other diversity analysis tools. At each k-mer position, distinct sequences are classified into motifs based on their incidence (frequency), revealing the composition and dynamics of sequence diversity.

## Motif Definitions

| Motif | Definition | Significance |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| **Index** | Most frequent k-mer at the position | Represents the prevalent wild-type/consensus sequence |
| **Major Variant** | Second most frequent k-mer | Primary alternative; often an emerging variant |
| **Minor Variants** | K-mers occurring more than once, below major | Intermediate diversity; background variation |
| **Unique Variants** | K-mers occurring exactly once (singletons) | May be sequencing artifacts or rare mutations |

## Classification Rules

Given $N$ valid k-mers at a position with $n$ distinct sequences ranked by count:

$$
\text{rank}_1 \geq \text{rank}_2 \geq \text{rank}_3 \geq \ldots \geq \text{rank}_n
$$

- **Index:** The sequence with the highest count ($\text{rank}_1$), provided count > 1
- **Major:** The sequence with the second-highest count ($\text{rank}_2$), provided count > 1
- **Minor:** All sequences with count > 1 and rank below major
- **Unique:** All sequences with count = 1

### Special Cases

- **Multiple indices** — If two or more sequences share the highest count, all are classified as index
- **Multiple majors** — If two or more sequences share the second-highest count, all are classified as major
- **No index** — If all sequences are unique (count = 1), there is no index or major
- **No major** — If only one sequence occurs more than once, there is no major variant

## Incidence Calculations

DiMA reports incidence as a percentage for each motif:

$$
\text{index\_incidence} = \frac{\text{index\_count}}{N} \times 100\%
$$

$$
\text{total\_variants\_incidence} = \frac{N - \text{index\_count}}{N} \times 100\%
$$

$$
\text{distinct\_variants\_incidence} = \frac{n - 1}{\text{total\_variant\_count}} \times 100\%
$$

Where:
- $N$ = total support (valid k-mers at position)
- $n$ = number of distinct sequences
- total_variant_count = sum of all non-index variant counts

<Aside type="note">
"Total variants" encompasses all variants that differ by at least one residue from the index. "Distinct variants" is the count of different sequences among the total variants.
</Aside>

## Example

Consider a nonamer position with 20 sequences:

| Sequence | Count | Motif |
|----------|-------|-------|
| ACDEFGHIK | 8 | **Index** (40%) |
| ACDEFGHIL | 5 | **Major** (25%) |
| ACDEFGHIV | 3 | Minor (15%) |
| ACDEFGHIM | 2 | Minor (10%) |
| ACDEFGHIW | 1 | Unique (5%) |
| ACDEFGHIY | 1 | Unique (5%) |

- Index incidence: 40%
- Total variants incidence: 60%
- Distinct variants: 5 (out of 12 total variant occurrences) = 41.7%

## Biological Significance

- **High index incidence** (&gt;80%) — Conserved region; potential vaccine/diagnostic target
- **Low index incidence** (&lt;50%) — Highly variable region; potential immune escape site
- **Dominant minor variants** — Suggests selective pressure with multiple competing lineages
- **High unique count** — May indicate sequencing artifacts or extreme hypervariability

## Motif Switching

A key finding from DiMA studies: the same sequence can change motif classification across adjacent k-mer positions due to the sliding window. A sequence that is the index at position $x$ may become a minor variant at position $x+1$ if a single residue change alters its relative frequency. This phenomenon, termed "motif switching," reveals the dynamic nature of sequence diversity at the amino acid level (Hu et al. 2013, *PLoS ONE*; Abd Raman et al. 2020, *PeerJ*).
